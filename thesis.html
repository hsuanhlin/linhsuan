About
I am interested in the movement within a manmade space. A few years ago, I first noticed that differentiating others' movements is a good way to locate myself while glaring spotlights beaming across my eyelids on runways. Another reconfiguration of movement is the time when I was photoshooting in a seamless man-made studio, I moved unintentionally in order to give a narrative to one confined image which defines by the agency of beauty.

What if the slight/bold movement we act everyday is based on a constructed protocol? What is the protocol look like? Mundane, as defined by the Cambridge Dictionary is “very ordinary and therefore not interesting,” (Cambridge Dictionary). Within the scope of social sciences and humanities, Mary Douglas further deconstructs the concept of ‘the mundane’ as “less a property of things than it is a contextual label attributed to them,” (Douglas, 1966). Specific to the sphere of design, Harry West describes this phenomenon as revealing “that sometimes it’s the most mundane and invisible that’s the key to seeing things in a new way,” (Harry West, the principal of Invisible Design).

According to Rem Koolhaas in his book, Delirious New York : A Retroactive Manifesto for Manhattan, he asserts that the primary intent of architecture must include all the performative activities of human occupants within a space. (Koolhaas, 1994). Dance, on the other hand, is defined as “the movement of the body in a rhythmic way, usually to music and within a given space” (Britannica).

By isolating humans, and their actions, within the constructs of manmade environments, I believe that protocol offers a unique opportunity to re-envision the movement by reexamining the interplay between humans and physical spaces.
